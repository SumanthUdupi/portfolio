Nobody warned me that a degree in mechanical engineering would one day explain everything about user interface design.

But here we are.

## The Problem With Straight Lines

Engineering teaches you something peculiar: the world is full of systems that *look* like they should behave linearly, and almost none of them do. A beam under load, a fluid through a pipe, a supply chain under pressure — all of them bend, turbulate, cascade. The job of the engineer is to account for that gap between the idealized model and the actual, messy, physical truth.

When I crossed over into data science, the lesson repeated itself in a different language. Your model is not the data. The features you chose are a story you told yourself about what matters. The accuracy metric you're optimizing hides twenty other things going wrong underneath.

And then I started designing interfaces for human beings.

> The user is not the persona. The workflow is not the flowchart. The mental model is not the information architecture.

It turns out the core skill — the thing that threads mechanical engineering, data science, and product design together — is this: **learning to see the gap between the map and the territory, and caring deeply enough to close it.**

## What Engineers Miss

Here is what my engineering background got wrong about design, for a long time: I thought the goal was correctness.

A bridge that doesn't fall. A calculation that checks out. A model that fits. There is something deeply satisfying about correctness. It is clean. It is falsifiable. It either works or it doesn't.

Design is messier than that. A button can be technically correct — right color contrast ratio, right placement according to every heuristic — and still feel wrong. There is a felt quality to a well-designed interaction that doesn't reduce to a checklist, and I spent an embarrassing amount of time being frustrated by that before I accepted it as true.

The philosopher in me eventually made peace with it: not everything real is measurable. Some things have to be *experienced* to be evaluated. That's not a failure of rigor. That's just the nature of the domain.

## What Philosophers Miss

Philosophy, on the other hand, taught me to love abstraction a little too much.

There is a kind of thinking that is exquisitely careful, deeply precise, beautifully structured — and completely detached from whether anything in the real world changes as a result. I have written essays that satisfied me enormously as intellectual objects and helped exactly nobody.

Product design is a corrective for this. Every abstraction has to be cashed out in pixels, flows, and real human behavior. The philosophy has to ship. The model has to run. The interface has to be used by a person who didn't read your design document and has three other tabs open.

This is clarifying in a way that pure thought rarely is.

## The Through-Line

So here is the honest version of my career, stripped of the buzzwords:

I spent years learning how to think about systems — physical ones, then statistical ones, then human ones. Each domain kept teaching me the same thing: *reality is more complicated than your current model, and the interesting work is in closing that gap with honesty and craft.*

Engineering gave me the tolerance for complexity. Data science gave me the respect for evidence. Design gave me the responsibility to make something a real human being actually wants to use.

None of them are separate disciplines to me anymore. They're the same practice, applied to different materials.

## Where This Leaves Me

I don't think "multidisciplinary" quite captures it. Multidisciplinary sounds like you're juggling several different things. What I'm describing feels more like the same thing, seen from several different angles.

The question I keep returning to: what does it mean to understand a system well enough to change it for the better? What counts as evidence? What counts as improvement? Who decides?

These are design questions. They're also engineering questions. They're also philosophy questions.

I'm not sure I'll ever stop finding that interesting.

<Takeaway>
The through-line between engineering, data science, and design isn't technical skill — it's a disposition toward the gap between how things are modeled and how they actually behave. That gap is where all the interesting work lives.
</Takeaway>
